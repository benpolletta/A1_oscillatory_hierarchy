CALLFRIEND Farsi


Introduction

This directory contains sample data from CALLFRIEND Farsi, Linguistic Data Consortium (LDC) catalog number LDC96S50 and isbn 1-58563-065-9.

CALLFRIEND Farsi was collected by LDC primarily in support of the Language Identification (LID) project sponsored by the U.S. Department of Defense. This corpus consists of 60 unscripted telephone conversations between native speakers of Farsi. The recorded conversations last up to 30 minutes, and participants were not restricted in the choice of their conversation topics. All speakers were aware that their conversations were recored for research. Once a caller was recruited to participate, he/she was given a free choice of whom to call. Most participants called family members or close friends. All calls originated in either the United States or Canada.

Farsi, also known as Persian, is the principal language of Iran and has a large diaspora of speakers throughout the world. There are roughly 23 million Farsi speakers worldwide. Farsi is an Indo-European language and a member of the West Iranian family of languages; it is closely related to Dari and Tajik. The current distribution of Farsi centers around Iran and extends to the neighboring Persian Gulf states. Historically, the range of the language was quite large and inlcuded portions of India and Mesopatamia. For that reason, Farsi influenced the development of the Turkic and Hindustani languages.

Data

Speakers were solicited by the LDC to participate in this telephone speech collection effort via the internet, publications (advertisements), and personal contacts. A total of 100 call originators were found, each of whom placed a telephone call via a toll-free robot operator maintained by the LDC. Access to the robot operator was possible via a unique Personal Identification Number (PIN) issued by the recruiting staff at the LDC when the caller enrolled in the project. The participants were made aware that their telephone call would be recorded, as were the call recipients. The call was allowed only if both parties agreed to being recorded. Each caller was allowed to talk up to 30 minutes. Upon successful completion of the call, the caller was paid $20 (in addition to making a free long-distance telephone call). Each caller was allowed to place only one telephone call. After a successful call was completed, a human audit of each telephone call was conducted to verify that the proper language was spoken and to check the quality of the recording.

Presented here are three two-minute samples from selected phone calls. The sample audio files are MS Wave files (.wav), but the actual corpus uses the NIST Sphere audio file format. Software to convert NIST to other formats is readily available through the LDC. The demographic information listed below describes the person placing the call in the samples.
